J-Stack is published to make agentic journalism and publication-safety workflows inspectable, reusable, and improvable. It is deliberately public, experimental, and adaptable. That openness does **not** make it a certified product, a legal opinion, a compliance framework, a security assessment, a newsroom policy, or professional advice.

Use J-Stack at your own responsibility.

## 1. Experimental open-source material

J-Stack is an experimental open-source skill collection and workflow library for AI-assisted journalism, investigation, public writing, evidence discipline, GBrain-backed memory, and publication safety.

The repository is a **technical template and starting point**. It is not a checked production system, not a guarantee of safe publication, and not a substitute for your own editorial, legal, technical, security, and compliance review.

## 7. LLM and agent-output risk

Large language models and autonomous agents can produce confident but false output. They can invent citations, misread documents, overstate evidence, omit counterevidence, miss deadlines, and generate unsafe wording.

For every real use, independently verify:

- statutes, regulations, case law, administrative materials, and citations;
- dates, deadlines, limitation periods, filing periods, and procedural requirements;
- names, titles, affiliations, addresses, URLs, and identifiers;
- numbers, calculations, statistics, datasets, and charts;
- quotes, summaries, translations, and document extractions;
- allegations, criminal/misconduct labels, causal claims, and other high-risk assertions;
- source authenticity, context, and publication permission.

No factual claim should ship above its evidence grade.

J-Stack is designed to keep consequential actions behind human gates.

Human approval remains mandatory before:

- making payments or contractual commitments;
- deploying systems or changing infrastructure;
- giving legal, compliance, tax, security, medical, financial, or other professional advice;

## 9. Testing and confidential material

Do not test with real confidential, privileged, source-sensitive, personal, client, or regulated data unless the environment is approved, documented, access-controlled, and legally reviewed for that purpose.

Prefer synthetic, public, redacted, or dummy data for testing. Keep GBrain and other memory systems concise and non-secret unless you have a clear governance model for sensitive storage.

## 10. Secrets hygiene

Never commit secrets to this repository or include them in issues, pull requests, screenshots, logs, examples, or test data.

This includes:

- API keys;
- access tokens;
- passwords;
- OAuth device codes;
- private keys;
- cookies and session data;
- provider logs containing credentials;
- screenshots that reveal credentials, internal systems, personal data, or confidential source material.

If a secret may have been exposed, revoke and rotate it through the relevant provider.

## 11. Academic and training misuse

If J-Stack or derivative skills are used for education, writing practice, research training, exam preparation, or classroom work, users must comply with the applicable academic rules.

Do not submit AI-generated output as your own work where that would be prohibited. Do not use J-Stack to fabricate sources, citations, interviews, datasets, or evidence. Learning workflows should support understanding, not deception.

## 12. Contributions and adaptations

J-Stack is meant to be inspected, forked, tested, criticized, and improved. If a skill is wrong, incomplete, jurisdiction-specific, too broad, too risky, or not useful for your workflow, adapt it and consider opening an issue or pull request.

The repository is a public starting point, not an authority.
